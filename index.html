01 — Field Guide

Crop Types

From staple grains to high-value cash crops, every crop family asks something different of the soil, season and farmer.

Cereals

Rice, wheat, maize and millets — the backbone of food security, grown across nearly every agro-climatic zone.

Staple grain

Pulses

Lentils, chickpeas and gram fix nitrogen in the soil while supplying most of the world's plant protein.

Nitrogen-fixing

Oilseeds

Groundnut, mustard and soybean are pressed for cooking oil and processed into protein-rich cattle feed.

Rabi & Kharif

Cash Crops

Cotton, sugarcane and jute are grown primarily for sale and export rather than on-farm consumption.

Commercial

Fruits

Mango, banana and citrus orchards need long-term investment but return steady, high-value harvests.

Horticulture

Vegetables

Tomato, onion and leafy greens are short-duration crops that keep cash flowing between main seasons.

Short duration
02 — Beneath Your Feet

Soil Classification

Soil is the first input any farmer works with. Knowing its type shapes every decision that follows — from crop choice to fertilizer dose.

Alluvial Soil

Deposited by rivers, rich in potash and lime — the most fertile soil, ideal for cereals and sugarcane.

River plains

Black (Regur) Soil

High clay content retains moisture well, making it the classic choice for cotton and oilseed cultivation.

Cotton belt

Red & Yellow Soil

Iron-rich and porous; responds well to irrigation and organic matter for pulses, millets and groundnut.

Iron-rich

Laterite Soil

Formed under heavy rainfall and leaching; needs lime and manure but suits cashew, tea and coffee.

Plantation

Arid & Desert Soil

Sandy, low in organic matter and moisture — needs drip irrigation and drought-hardy crop varieties.

Low rainfall

Mountain & Forest Soil

Thin, humus-rich topsoil found on slopes; supports tea, spices and terraced horticulture.

Terraced
03 — Water Management

Irrigation Techniques

Getting water to the root zone efficiently is often the biggest lever on yield — and on the water bill.

Drip Irrigation

Delivers water directly to the root zone drop by drop, cutting water use by up to 60% versus flooding.

Water-efficient

Sprinkler Irrigation

Mimics rainfall through overhead nozzles — well suited to undulating land and closely spaced crops.

Uniform coverage

Flood Irrigation

The traditional method of letting water spread freely across a field — low-cost but water-intensive.

Low-cost

Furrow Irrigation

Water runs through narrow channels between crop rows, keeping foliage dry and reducing disease risk.

Row crops

Subsurface Irrigation

Buried pipes feed water below the surface directly to roots, minimising evaporation loss entirely.

Minimal loss

Rainwater Harvesting

Farm ponds and check dams capture monsoon runoff, building a water reserve for the dry season ahead.

Monsoon capture
04 — Feeding the Soil

Fertilizers & Nutrients

Balanced nutrition — not maximum quantity — is what turns healthy soil into a healthy harvest.

Nitrogen (N)

Drives leafy, vegetative growth and chlorophyll production — the nutrient crops need in the largest amount.

Vegetative growth

Phosphorus (P)

Strengthens root development and speeds up flowering, fruiting and overall energy transfer in the plant.

Root & flower

Potassium (K)

Improves disease resistance, water regulation and grain quality — often called the "quality nutrient".

Disease resistance

Organic & Compost

Farmyard manure, vermicompost and green manure rebuild soil structure and long-term fertility.

Soil health

Biofertilizers

Living microbial cultures like Rhizobium and Azotobacter fix nutrients naturally, cutting chemical dependence.

Microbial

Micronutrients

Zinc, boron and iron are needed in tiny amounts but their absence quietly caps every other input's return.

Trace elements
05 — Crop Protection

Pest Management

The best pest strategy blends prevention, biology and chemistry — reaching for the sprayer last, not first.

Integrated Pest Management

Combines monitoring, thresholds and multiple tactics to keep pests in check with minimal chemical input.

IPM

Biological Control

Natural predators like ladybirds and parasitic wasps keep pest populations down without any spraying.

Natural predators

Chemical Control

Targeted pesticides used at the right dose and stage — a fast fix reserved for real outbreaks.

Targeted use

Cultural Practices

Crop rotation, timed sowing and field sanitation break pest life cycles before they ever take hold.

Prevention

Botanical Pesticides

Neem and pyrethrum extracts offer effective, biodegradable protection with low toxicity to humans.

Bio-pesticide

Pheromone Traps

Scent lures draw in and trap specific insect species, useful for both monitoring and mass control.

Monitoring
06 — Support Systems

Government Agricultural Schemes

Key national programmes that provide income support, insurance, credit and infrastructure to farmers.

PM-KISAN

Direct income support of ₹6,000 per year to eligible farmer families, paid in three instalments.

Income Support

PM Fasal Bima Yojana

Low-premium crop insurance protecting farmers against yield loss from natural calamities and pests.

PMFBY

Soil Health Card Scheme

Free periodic soil testing that recommends the right nutrient dose for every individual farm plot.

Soil Testing

Kisan Credit Card

Affordable, flexible credit for seeds, fertilizer and equipment at concessional interest rates.

KCC

PM Krishi Sinchayee Yojana

Expands irrigation coverage and promotes efficient, on-farm water use through modern methods.

PMKSY

e-NAM

An online trading platform connecting mandis nationwide, giving farmers fairer price discovery.

Digital Market
